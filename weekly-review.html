The Weekly Review

45 minutes every Friday. The single ritual that prevents drift, closes loops, and pre-loads the next week.

Why This Ritual Is Non-Negotiable

The Compounding Return on Review

Most productivity loss doesn't happen in execution — it happens in the gaps between weeks, when open loops accumulate, priorities silently shift, and the connection between daily work and long-term goals erodes. The weekly review is the antidote: it processes accumulated noise, realigns compass bearing, and converts the coming week from reactive to intentional.

At PhD level and above, the review has an additional function: it maintains the research narrative. Progress on long-horizon projects is not visible day-to-day. The weekly review is the granularity at which direction and momentum become legible.

Compounding effect: A researcher who does 48 weekly reviews per year has 48 course corrections. One who skips them drifts for weeks before noticing. Over a 5-year PhD, this difference compounds into months of effective work time.

Phase-by-Phase

The Full Protocol

1
Capture Clear
Process all open inboxes to zero
5 min

Before you can review the week, you must clear the accumulated inputs. This phase is mechanical: move everything from capture points into your system or delete it. Do not get distracted reading deeply — just triage.

  • Physical inbox / notebook pages→ transcribe any actionable items into your task system
  • Email inbox → zero→ archive, respond (if <2min), or create task. Not both.
  • Slack / messaging DMs→ clear or snooze. Don't leave open loops.
  • Browser tabs (open = open loop)→ save to read-later app or close. Not both.
  • Desktop / Downloads folder→ file or delete. Zero loose files.
2
Week Retrospective
What actually happened vs. what was planned
10 min

Read back through the week's daily logs — specifically the WIN / ADJUST / TOMORROW lines from each shutdown. Do not reconstruct from memory. Memory is biased toward recency and drama. The logs are the data.

Then calculate your MIT completion rate for the week. This number is the primary signal of system health. Track it over time.

Week Retro Template
Week: [YYYY-WW]
MITs planned: ___ · completed: ___ · rate: ___%

Biggest output this week: [concrete deliverable]
Biggest miss: [what didn't happen, be specific]
Root cause of miss: [overcommitment / distraction / unclear scope / external block]

Energy pattern: [Mon–Fri 1–5, e.g. 4/3/5/4/3]
Pattern I notice: [one honest observation]
# If MIT rate < 50% two weeks running → system recalibration needed
3
Project Status Sweep
Every active project gets a 60-second status update
10 min

Open your project list (should be ≤ 10 active projects at any time). For each project: update status, check if on track vs. original milestone dates. The goal is not detailed planning — it's early warning detection.

🟢 On Track
Progress this week visible. Milestone dates intact. Next action clear. No action needed in review — just log status.
🟡 At Risk
Progress slower than planned, or external dependency uncertain. Flag for Monday planning — needs dedicated time or explicit decision to descope.
🔴 Behind
Milestone already missed or clearly unreachable. Must either: reset milestone with honest date, reduce scope, or officially pause project. Don't let it linger.
⬜ Stalled (>3 weeks)
No progress for 3+ consecutive weeks. Decision required: archive, delegate, or restart with new constraints. Stalled projects are cognitive debt.
4
OKR Check
Update quarterly score estimates, check alignment
5 min

For each Key Result, update your current score estimate (0.0–1.0). Then ask the single most important alignment question.

OKR Weekly Check
Quarter: [Q3 2025] · Week: [8 of 13]

O1: [Objective text]
KR1: 0.__ → 0.__ (was / now)
KR2: 0.__ → 0.__

Alignment check: [Did this week's DW blocks move at least one KR? Y/N]
If N → next week DW1 assigned to: [specific KR]
# Projected end-of-quarter score based on current trajectory?
# If projected < 0.4 → escalate to monthly review agenda
5
Next Week Pre-Load
Design next week while this week is still in memory
15 min

This phase has the highest leverage. Pre-loading next week on Friday means Monday begins with clarity instead of planning overhead. The decision is made when your context is richest — right after reviewing the week.

  • Open Monday Planning template, fill in Week's Single Most Important Outcome→ one sentence. If you do nothing else next week, do this.
  • Pre-schedule both Deep Work blocks in calendar for Mon and Tue→ assign specific projects to each block before Monday arrives
  • Review all meetings already on next week's calendar→ cancel or shorten anything that doesn't require your presence
  • Identify the single highest-leverage task for each active project→ these become the candidate MIT pool for the week
  • Pre-commit one "avoid" — a specific distraction or low-value activity to decline→ pre-commitment decisions cost less willpower than in-the-moment ones
Next Week Pre-Load Output
Week's single outcome: [fill here on Friday]
DW1 Mon assigned to: [project + specific task]
DW1 Tue assigned to: [project + specific task]
Must avoid: [specific]
Open risk: [one thing that could derail the week]

System Health Indicators

Interpreting Your Review Data

Track these numbers over 4+ weeks to diagnose system health:

MIT Rate > 80%
System working well. Consider increasing ambition of MITs.
MIT Rate 50–80%
Normal operating range. Identify single biggest category of misses and address.
MIT Rate < 50%
System signal: MITs too ambitious, scheduling broken, or external load too high. Reduce before restoring.
0 DW sessions/week
Critical failure mode. Deep work is the only mechanism of progress on hard problems. Everything else is maintenance.
