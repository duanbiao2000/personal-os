The Quarterly Review

2 hours, four times a year. The highest-leverage planning session in the system — recalibrates direction before drift becomes damage.

The Three-Level Review Hierarchy

How Reviews Stack

Reviews operate at three temporal resolutions. Each level asks a different question and requires different cognitive mode. Confusing them — doing strategic thinking at weekly review cadence, or doing tactical planning at the quarterly — is a common failure mode.

Weekly · Every Friday
Navigation
Am I executing the plan? What's working this week? Close loops, pre-load next week.
45m
Monthly · First Friday
Calibration
Is the plan still right? Pattern recognition across 4 weeks. OKR trajectory check. One adjustment allowed.
+30m
Quarterly · End of Q
Recalibration
Are my goals still the right goals? Full OKR close + new OKR set. Strategic horizon reset.
2h
The quarterly review is the only place in the system where you are permitted to change your Objectives. Changing direction mid-quarter based on weekly review data is a sign of instability, not responsiveness. Resist it. Log the impulse, bring it to the quarterly.

2-Hour Protocol

Block by Block

0:00
Setup & Context Loading (10 min)
Offline. No interruptions. Have open: last quarter's OKRs, all weekly review logs for the quarter, your project list, your long-term vision document (if you have one). Make coffee. This is a thinking session, not an admin session.
0:10
OKR Post-Mortem (30 min)
Score each Key Result honestly (0.0–1.0). Then go deeper: why did you hit or miss? The score is not the point — the causal story behind the score is. What do you now know about your constraints, capacity, and prioritization that you didn't know at the start of the quarter?
0:40
Quarter Retrospective — The Four Questions (30 min)
The highest-leverage 30 minutes in your system. Slow down here. These questions are designed to surface the signal beneath the noise of 13 weeks of activity.
1:10
Next Quarter OKR Design (30 min)
Draft new Objectives and Key Results informed by post-mortem insights. This is the creative phase. Don't anchor too heavily on last quarter's structure — allow genuine recalibration.
1:40
System Audit & Commitment (20 min)
Review the Personal OS itself. What friction points accumulated over the quarter? Which templates need updating? Make one concrete system improvement. Then write your quarterly commitment — a single paragraph you'll re-read at next quarter's review.

The Four Questions

Retrospective Deep Dive

These questions are ordered by depth. Spend at least 5 minutes per question. Write answers, don't just think them — writing forces precision.

Question 01
What did I actually spend my time on, versus what I said I would?
This is a reality check, not a judgment. Most people dramatically overestimate how much time they spent on their stated priorities and underestimate time spent on reactive work. The calendar data doesn't lie. Go look at it.
Question 02
What created the most value — and was it planned or emergent?
Some of the best outcomes of the quarter will have been unplanned. Identifying these tells you something about where your comparative advantage actually lies, which should inform next quarter's Objectives. Don't over-optimize against what you planned; optimize toward what generated real results.
Question 03
Is how I spent this quarter aligned with what I care about on a 5-year horizon?
This is the only strategic question in the system. Everything else is operational. If the answer is no — or if you can't answer it — that's the most important signal from the quarter, and the primary input to next quarter's Objective design.
Question 04
What should I stop doing, start doing, and continue doing?
The classic retrospective framework, but taken seriously at the quarterly level. "Stop" items are often harder than "start" — they require saying no to commitments that feel safe or socially expected. The quarterly review is the right forum for those decisions.

Next Quarter OKR Design

The Design Process

Step 1 · Horizon Check
Where do you want to be in 12 months?
"If I look back from a year from now, what would make this period feel like a success?" Write this before looking at last quarter's OKRs.
Step 2 · Constraint Mapping
What is actually non-negotiable this quarter?
Deadlines, commitments, external obligations. Map them first — then design Objectives that are ambitious within real constraints, not despite them.
Step 3 · Objective Draft
1–3 Objectives, qualitative and inspiring
Each Objective should answer: "Why would completing this matter in 5 years?" If you can't answer that, reconsider the Objective.
Step 4 · KR Stress Test
Are these Key Results actually measurable?
For each KR: Can you score it 0.0 / 0.3 / 0.7 / 1.0 with clear definitions? If not, rewrite. Ambiguous KRs produce ambiguous accountability.

Common Mistakes

What Breaks Quarterly Reviews

Mistake 01
Doing the quarterly in the same headspace as weekly reviews
Weekly reviews are operational. Quarterly reviews require a different cognitive mode — more reflective, less reactive. Change your environment. Do it on a Saturday morning, not a Friday afternoon between meetings.
Mistake 02
Carrying forward last quarter's OKRs unchanged
If nothing changed, you didn't review — you rescheduled. The quarterly is specifically designed to allow genuine recalibration. Use it.
Mistake 03
Treating low scores as failure rather than signal
A 0.4 score on a genuinely ambitious KR is more valuable data than a 1.0 on an easy one. The quarterly review is diagnostic, not performative. No one is grading you — you're building a calibration model of yourself.
